# Source Evaluation

Use this reference to decide how much weight to give each source and how much uncertainty to carry into the final report.

## Source Classes

### Primary
- official company blogs
- documentation
- changelogs
- product pages
- repositories and release notes
- regulatory filings
- official interviews, talks, or transcripts

Use as the baseline for facts about launches, availability, pricing, and product claims.

### Secondary
- reputable media coverage
- analyst writeups
- industry publications
- well-sourced newsletters

Use for context, interpretation, and synthesis. Confirm important claims against primary evidence when possible.

### Commentary
- opinion columns
- founder or investor hot takes
- social threads
- editorial reactions

Use as signal about sentiment or framing, not as proof.

### Promotional
- sponsored posts
- affiliate content
- clipped marketing videos
- sales collateral
- “explainer” content produced to drive hype

Treat as high-risk. Extract claims, then verify elsewhere.

## Reliability Questions

Ask:
- Is this first-hand or derivative?
- Does it provide evidence, examples, or data?
- Does it report facts, interpret them, or sell a narrative?
- What incentive does the publisher have to exaggerate?
- Does it omit tradeoffs, limitations, or availability constraints?
- Does it use exact dates, product names, versions, prices, or metrics?
- Can the key claim be traced to a primary source?

## Special Rules for YouTube and Short-Form Video

- A video can be a lead, not automatic proof.
- Distinguish between clips that report news and clips that react to it.
- If the creator cites a source, follow that source before trusting the creator's summary.
- If the video lacks transcript, timestamped evidence, or source links, reduce confidence.

## Red Flags

- no source link for a strong claim
- no publication date for a time-sensitive assertion
- anonymous sourcing with no corroboration
- sensational title with weak substance
- launch language without evidence of availability
- competitive claims without benchmarks or methodology
- pricing claims without official pricing pages

## Confidence Guidance

### High confidence
- core claim supported by primary sources
- important numbers and dates match across sources
- no major contradictions remain

### Medium confidence
- credible sources align, but some important details remain indirect or incomplete

### Low confidence
- evidence comes mainly from commentary, clipped video, rumors, or marketing language
- key claims cannot be traced to first-hand documentation
